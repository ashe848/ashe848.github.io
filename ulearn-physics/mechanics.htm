Linear/Translational Motion

This is about the motion of an object as a whole

Scalar quantities have magnitude but no direction e.g. time, degrees Celsius
Vector quantities have both magnitude and direction e.g. F (5N to the left), velocity (3 metres per second at 20 degrees northwest from the road)

Newton's Laws of Motion
1st Law: If an object is in equilibrium (Fnett=0), it is either stationary or travelling at a constant velocity.
2nd Law: An unbalanced force (Fnett doesn't =0) causes acceleration in the same direction. F=ma.
3rd Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. I.e. if A exerts a force on B, B exerts the same amount of force back on A.

Force (Resultant/nett/unbalanced force)
Vector addition is performed by arranging the arrows head-to-tail, then finding the resultant (the hypotenuse) through Pythagoras.

Example 1: if the cart is only moving left, not into the ground, vertical forces balanced. Fup = Fw + Fvert from pushing, and Fhor = Funb = Fresultant from pushing.
Example 2: If an object is lifted straight up, Funb is upwards. Therefore, Fup = Fw (to balance out mg) + Funb (does the lifting)

Vector "subtraction" means adding the negative of the second vector. I.e. A-B = A+(-B) = A plus Bin the opposite direction

Momentum
p=mv in kgms-1

In an isolated system, there are no external forces, momentum is conserved according to the Principle of Conservation of Momentum. (Friction is commonly neglected)
pi = pf
MVi + mvi = MVf + mvf (vectorial addition)


If in a straight line, change in pA = change in pB in the opposite direction.

Remember: momentum is conserved, but velocity is almost NEVER conserved!

Impulse
Impulse is change in momentum. Δp; = pf - pi = pf + pi in opposite direction = mvf - mvi in opposite direction.

F = ma
F = m Δv;/Δt;
FΔt; = mΔv; = Δp;

Centre of Mass
The point which behaves as if all the mass of an object or system is concentrated at that point.
It doesn't have to be inside the object, for example, the Moon and Earth both orbit about a centre of mass of the system.

From a common reference point, (r is distance from reference point)
m1r1 + m2r2 + ... + mnrn = (m1+m2+...+mn) rCoM

Centre of Mass and Conservation of Momentum together: psystem = (M+m) vcom
p is conserved, total mass doesn't change, ∴ vcom is constant.

If centre of mass was stationary, it will remain stationary.
Example 1: If a stationary object explodes, the pieces will fly off in various directions at various speeds, such that the centre of mass stays where is was in the beginning.
pA + pB + pC = 0, ∴ pA = -pB + -pC. Remember vectorial addition!

Example 2: If a parcel dropped from a flying plane splits, the centre of mass will continue to travel with vcom in projectile motion, and "land" where the parcel would have if it hadn't split.


Energy
Note the energy formulae in the formula sheet

Principle of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, but changed from one form to another.

Elastic collision: total energy of objects remain unchanged (as well as momentum).
Inelastic collision: energy usually lost from the system as heat from friction.

For the purpose of L3 Physics, friction is neglected most of the time.
Etotal = Ei = Ef = Ek + Ep

Work
W = F d (the unit Joules is equivalent to Newton metres)

If work is done against gravity (e.g. lifting something up)
W = Fg dup = mg h = E
∴ work done = Gravitational PE gained.

Kinematic Equations
For motion in a straight line with constant acceleration.

Vf = Vi + at
Vf2 = Vi2 + 2ad
d = 0.5 (Vf + Vi) t
d = Vit + 0.5at2
d = Vft - 0.5at2
